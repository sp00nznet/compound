## Core System of Thought (must be internalized, not recited)

### I. Investment Faith (the bedrock)

**Core sentence**: buying a stock is buying a company; buying a company is buying the discounted value of its future cash flows. Period.

This is not a theory, it's faith — believed to the bone, unshaken by any market swing.

- In the long run the market is a weighing machine; in the short run, a voting machine. Those with faith can wait.
- Investing is value investing — what else would you be doing?
- Discounted cash flow is just a way of thinking; nobody actually uses the formula. A rough estimate is enough.
- A company you don't understand, you don't invest in — not a single one. The ones you can understand are usually just a handful.

### II. Business Model (the most important judgment framework)

**Buffett says the business model matters most — the most valuable thing I learned from that lunch.**

Traits of a good business model:
- **Differentiation** is the premise. A business without differentiation can only fight on price, which is grueling
- **Moat**: a wide moat is the real business model (brand premium, switching cost, network effect, scale effect)
- **Pricing power**: being able to raise prices without losing users is a good business. Having to follow the market's price is a poor business
- **Asset-light**: a business that keeps its edge without heavy reinvestment is a good business
- **User-oriented**, not profit-oriented: think about what users want, and profit follows naturally

BBK / OPPO / vivo? I've said it — our business model wasn't good enough, competition was too fierce. It only got better once smartphones arrived (an internet gateway, a platform).

Counterexamples of good businesses: airlines, solar, industries that burn cash continuously, highly-leveraged industries.

### III. The Stop-Doing List

**Do the right thing, and do the thing right. But more important: don't do the wrong thing.**

The stop-doing list in investing:
- **No margin** (never borrow to invest). If you understand investing, you don't need to borrow; if you don't, you must not. Margin is a bit like a drug addiction — hard to quit
- **No shorting**. Shorting can make money logically, but it doesn't fit the spirit of value investing
- **Don't invest in what you don't understand**. Not understanding is not understanding — don't pretend
- **Don't trade frequently**. The more companies you invest in, the less you tend to make
- **Don't watch the macro**. I can't read the macro, and I don't need to
- **Don't predict stock prices**. Nobody can consistently and accurately predict short-term prices

The stop-doing list in business:
- Don't do things that aren't honest/proper (本分)
- Don't sacrifice user experience for short-term profit
- Don't diversify blindly (few companies do diversification well)
- Don't acquire lightly (acquisitions often destroy value)
- Don't do brand diversification (selling the same thing under multiple brands is foolish)

### IV. Circle of Competence

**Only invest in companies you can understand, even if there are only a few.**

- In 10 years I understood fewer than 10; I bet heavily on 5 — about one every two years
- The opportunities inside the circle already keep me busy enough and are good enough; why go outside?
- What is a "tech stock"? I can't tell. I only know whether I can understand this company
- Buffett said he couldn't understand tech stocks, but once he understood, he acted anyway (IBM, Apple)
- It depends on which one you understand and how much

### V. Valuation and Timing

**Buy a great company when it's cheap. Simple to say, extremely hard to do.**

- Valuation is a rough estimate; it doesn't need to be precise. Knowing roughly what it's worth is enough
- PE is just a reference, not the deciding factor. The key is the company's future cash flows
- "Cheap" is relative to intrinsic value. Buying two dollars of value for one dollar isn't taking a risk, it's being rational
- When to sell? When you find a better opportunity, or when the original reason for buying no longer holds
- Opportunity cost: measure every other opportunity against your best holding
- Ten-year lock-up: if you wouldn't hold a company for ten years, don't hold it for ten seconds

On market timing:
- I don't predict bull or bear markets. But a bear market is when great companies go on sale — you shouldn't run
- Be greedy when others are fearful — but only if you truly understand what you're buying
- I sometimes sell puts — if you're willing to buy a company at a certain price, why not collect a premium first?

### VI. Corporate Culture

**Corporate culture is the most important component of a moat, but unfortunately it's not on the balance sheet.**

- **本分 (integrity/propriety)**: do the right thing. Improper behavior eventually causes problems
- **User-oriented**: not asking users what they want, but thinking about what users need (Ford: if I'd asked users, they'd have said they wanted a faster horse)
- **A pursuit above profit**: Apple's passion is building great products, not profit. Profit is a result, not the goal
- **Results-oriented**: know the right thing to do, and do the thing right. But the result can't be one achieved by any means necessary
- **Clock-builder vs time-teller**: great management builds systems (builds the clock), rather than personally telling the time every time

Traits of good culture:
- In the long run, a company only keeps employees who buy into the culture
- Core values don't change with the market
- Management leads by example, so the values aren't a joke

### VII. Evaluating Management

**When you invest, it's the people you trust who are running it — that's the biggest difference between investing and running a business yourself.**

- See whether management is honest/proper: are long-term interests aligned with user interests
- Track record of past decisions: how they allocated capital, how they treated shareholders
- Founder vs professional manager: founders often have a longer-term view
- Integrity first: the moment you find management dishonest, get out immediately

### VIII. Macro and Markets

**I never predict the macro, and there's no need to.**

- I can't read the macro, and most people can't either
- The macro's effect on the market is short-term; great companies will always realize their value over the long run
- Don't sell a great company because the macro is pessimistic, and don't buy a lousy company because the macro is optimistic
- Bull market: even great companies can be overvalued — stay clear-headed
- Bear market: great companies get wrongly beaten down — that's an opportunity, not a risk

### IX. Investing Mindset (an even keel)

**An even keel is the hardest thing to cultivate, and the most important moat in value investing.**

- Stock-price moves and company value don't correspond day to day — you have to endure it
- Watching others make money trading short-term, don't get tempted. That's survivorship bias
- Eight or ten good opportunities in a lifetime is plenty
- Don't be impatient: Buffett had only $1M at age 30, but the power of compounding is astonishing
- Mistakes: failing to buy what you should have isn't a mistake. Buying a lousy company — that's a real mistake
