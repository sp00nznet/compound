## Step 2: Business Essence Analysis — Duan Yongping's "Right Business"

### One-Sentence Definition

**Rocket Lab's business essence is: a "full-stack supplier" of space infrastructure — designing and manufacturing satellite components and satellites for customers, then launching them into orbit on its own rockets.**

If forced into a more colloquial analogy: **the Foxconn + SF Express of space — we build your stuff, then we deliver it up there.**

### Business Model Canvas

Rocket Lab is not a "launch company" — launch is only 33% of revenue. At its core it is a **space systems integrator**:

1. **Space Systems (67% of revenue)**: reaction wheels, star trackers, solar cells, separation systems, laser communication terminals, flight software, satellite platforms. Built through 7 acquisitions, covering nearly every critical link in satellite manufacturing.
2. **Launch Services (33% of revenue)**: the Electron small rocket (300kg to LEO) and the Neutron medium rocket (13 tonnes to LEO, maiden flight in 2026 Q4).
3. **Potential third leg**: the SDA missile-defense satellite constellation ($816M contract), upgrading from "selling components" to "delivering whole satellites + on-orbit operations."

**Revenue model**: project-based (satellite manufacturing) + per-launch fees (launch services) + long-term operations & maintenance contracts. Not subscription-based, but government contracts provide multi-year revenue visibility.

### Gross Margin Analysis

GAAP gross margin rose from -3% in FY2021 to 34.4% in FY2025, and further to 38.2% in Q1 2026. Non-GAAP gross margin is 43%.

**Peer comparison**:
- Traditional aerospace & defense (Northrop Grumman, L3Harris): 25–30% gross margin
- SpaceX (private, estimated): launch-business gross margin estimated at 50%+ (reuse economics)
- Rocket Lab's 34% gross margin sits "better than traditional defense, worse than SpaceX"

Drivers of continued margin expansion: (1) Space Systems carries a high revenue share with better profitability; (2) higher Electron launch cadence amortizes fixed costs; (3) vertical integration lowers procurement costs.

### Operating Leverage Analysis

Revenue grew from $62M in FY2021 to $602M in FY2025 (~10x), while net loss widened only from -$117M to -$198M (~1.7x). Operating margin improved from -164% to -38%. This shows **operating leverage is being released** — revenue is growing much faster than expenses.

But FY2025 free cash flow deteriorated sharply to -$322M (vs. -$116M in FY2024), driven mainly by Neutron R&D capex rising from $67M to $156M. This is a textbook feature of an "investment phase."

### Duan Yongping–Style Questions

**What's good about this business? If you could describe it in one sentence, what would it be?**

> What's good is that "building space infrastructure" is a megatrend of extremely high certainty over the next 10–20 years, and Rocket Lab is the only company besides SpaceX that can offer a full-stack service "from components to satellites to launch." The core advantage of this business is the **cross-selling ability and customer lock-in created by vertical integration** — a customer who uses your reaction wheels will very likely also buy your solar cells, and ultimately launch on your rocket.

**But this business also has obvious "bad" sides**:
- Not subscription-based; no certainty of recurring revenue
- Capital-intensive; requires continuous large outlays
- Faces SpaceX, arguably the most formidable competitor in history
- Still unprofitable; dependent on capital-market financing

## Step 3: Moat Assessment — Buffett's "Economic Moat"

### Verifying the Five Moat Types One by One

| Moat Type | Assessment | Strength | Evidence |
|-----------|------|------|------|
| **Brand / pricing power** | Moderate | ★★★☆☆ | Electron's per-launch price is ~$7.5M, below SpaceX rideshare but above SpaceX's marginal cost. Pricing power is limited; SpaceX's price is the industry ceiling |
| **Switching costs** | Fairly strong | ★★★★☆ | Satellite components (reaction wheels, star trackers, etc.), once designed in, are extremely costly to replace (requiring re-qualification, taking 6–18 months). Launch service itself has low switching costs |
| **Network effects** | Weak | ★★☆☆☆ | No network effect in the traditional sense. The "ecosystem effect" from vertical integration is not the same as a network effect |
| **Scale economies** | Moderate | ★★★☆☆ | The Electron factory has annual capacity of 52 vehicles; 21 launches in 2025 implies ~40% utilization. Scale growth amortizes cost, but the gap to SpaceX is enormous |
| **Technology / patent barriers** | Fairly strong | ★★★★☆ | Fully 3D-printed Rutherford electric-pump engine, carbon-composite rocket body, SolAero high-efficiency space solar cells (powering 1,000+ spacecraft). Technologically leading but not impossible to replicate |

### Moat Trajectory

**Past 5 years: clearly widening**
- Transformed from a pure rocket company into a full-stack space company, building vertical-integration capability through 7 acquisitions
- Backlog grew from nothing to $2.2B
- Government-contract credentials went from zero to selection for NSSL Phase 3 Lane 1 ($5.6B total program)
- Became prime contractor on the SDA missile-defense satellite program

**Next 5 years: hinges on Neutron**
- If Neutron succeeds: the moat widens substantially — becoming one of only two reusable orbital launch companies in the world (the other being SpaceX)
- If Neutron fails / is heavily delayed: the moat erodes — Electron gets squeezed between SpaceX rideshare and new entrants like Firefly

### One Moat Not to Ignore: the ITAR Regime

The U.S. International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR) effectively prohibit sensitive space payloads from launching on Chinese/Russian rockets. This creates an **institutionally protected market** for U.S. launch providers like Rocket Lab. Western defense and intelligence payloads can only choose U.S. or a few allied-nation launchers — a barrier Chinese commercial rocket companies cannot cross.

### Buffett–Style Questions

**Will this moat still be here in 10 years? What could destroy it?**

> The Space Systems (satellite-component) moat is the most durable — high switching costs, long qualification cycles, and Rocket Lab has already become the industry-standard supplier for several critical components. The launch-business moat is more fragile — if SpaceX's Starship achieves ultra-low-cost launch, it could push the entire small-and-medium rocket market to the margins.
>
> Forces that could destroy the moat: (1) SpaceX cutting prices sharply, making dedicated small-launch economically irrational; (2) Neutron failing, trapping Rocket Lab in the small-rocket niche; (3) Chinese commercial rockets breaking through the ITAR constraint and grabbing share in non-U.S. markets.

### Historical Analogies

| Analog Company | Similarities | Outcome |
|----------|--------|------|
| **Early SpaceX (2008–2012)** | Rocket startup, founder-driven, the leap from small to large rockets | Success. But SpaceX had Musk's personal fortune as a backstop; Rocket Lab can only rely on capital markets |
| **Virgin Orbit** | Small-rocket launch, space startup | Bankruptcy. The technical path (air-launch) was flawed and the cash ran out |
| **Iridium (1990s vs. 2010s)** | Satellite constellation buildout; failed and went bankrupt the first time, succeeded the second | The second generation became profitable. Shows timing is critical for space projects |
| **Boeing Starliner** | A legacy giant tackling a space project | Repeated failures, overtaken by SpaceX. Shows execution matters more than brand |

### Interdisciplinary Analysis

- **Technology adoption curve**: the space sector is on the steep upslope of the S-curve (similar to the internet in 1998–2002), an infrastructure-buildout phase. The risk is that in the 2000 internet bubble, many "right-direction" companies still lost money for investors due to excessive valuations
- **Competitive game theory**: SpaceX is an "asymmetric competitor" — its internal demand loop from Starlink lets it sustain high launch cadence without external customers. Rocket Lab depends entirely on external customers, leaving it weak in the pricing game
- **Survivorship bias**: we see Rocket Lab survive and thrive, but over the same period Virgin Orbit went bankrupt, Astra teetered on delisting, and ABL pivoted away from rockets. Rocket Lab's success doesn't mean "space startups" are a good business — it shows precisely that this is an industry with an extremely high washout rate

### Bias Self-Check

- **Narrative bias**: the "stars and oceans" space narrative is highly contagious, prone to making analysts overestimate the industry's prospects and the company's odds of success
- **Anchoring effect**: SpaceX's $1.77T IPO valuation may become an anchor, making RKLB's $55B valuation "look cheap" — but the two companies differ vastly in scale and maturity
- **SpaceX proxy effect**: how much of RKLB's valuation premium is the substitution effect of "can't buy SpaceX, so buy RKLB"? After SpaceX goes public, this portion of the premium could evaporate quickly

### Munger–Style Questions

**Where am I most likely to be wrong? Why would smart people not buy / short this company?**

> The most likely mistake: **being anchored to the industry's growth prospects while ignoring how absurd the valuation is**. A P/S of 91x means that even if Rocket Lab tripled revenue to $1.8B over the next 3 years, P/S would still be 30x — still expensive. A smart short would say: (1) Neutron's success is far from certain; (2) RKLB's "proxy premium" will disappear once SpaceX lists; (3) the $3B ATM dilution plan signals that management itself thinks the stock is overvalued. Bleecker Street Research is already short, with the core thesis that the Neutron timeline is sugar-coated and the only public contract comes from an unproven startup.

### The Company's Position in the Industry Value Chain

Rocket Lab occupies the **midstream** position in the space value chain:
- Upstream: raw materials, chips, basic components (not involved)
- **Midstream: satellite-component manufacturing + satellite integration + launch services (Rocket Lab's position)**
- Downstream: satellite operations, data services, communication services (Starlink, etc.)

The advantage of this position is that it is **a necessary passage for industry growth** — no matter who wins downstream, the midstream "seller of picks and shovels" benefits. The disadvantage is that margins are lower than downstream platform businesses (like Starlink), and it faces the complex "supplier and competitor at the same time" relationship.

### Li Lu–Style Questions

**Looking back 20 years from now, will this company be "the Standard Oil of its era" or "a flash-in-the-pan 3Com"?**

> The most likely outcome is neither Standard Oil nor 3Com, but **"the Airbus of the space industry"** — under SpaceX's dominance (the Boeing analog), becoming the world's second-largest space-infrastructure company, holding 15–25% market share, with its own strongholds in defense and specific commercial niches. That outcome would be good enough, but the question is: **has the current $55B valuation already fully — even excessively — priced in this "number two" value?**
