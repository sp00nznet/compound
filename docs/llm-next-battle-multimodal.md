# The Next Battle of LLMs: Is Multimodal Inevitable, or an Overhyped Narrative?

An in-depth dialectic on the future direction of AI

---

## Introduction: An Unavoidable Battle Over Direction

In May 2024, OpenAI released GPT-4o. At the launch event, when the model analyzed facial expressions in real time and responded to questions in an emotionally rich voice, the audience gasped. That moment forged a near-instinctive consensus across the global tech community: the future of large models lies in multimodality.

But is that consensus correct?

When we discuss "future direction," we are actually conflating two distinct questions: whether multimodality has value (almost everyone agrees it does); and whether multimodality should be treated as the top-priority main direction right now—even to the point of using it as a reason to downplay deep optimization of pure text language models (this is where genuine disagreement lies).

This article attempts to disentangle these two questions and, drawing on the technical progress, product cases, and market data of recent years, offer a more honest judgment.

---

## Dimension One: The Real Value of Expanded Perception

The most intuitive argument for prioritizing multimodality is that human cognition itself is multimodal.

This argument is not empty. In healthcare, Google's Med-PaLM M reaches specialist-level performance in diagnosing chest X-rays, fundus images, and pathology slides, and Stanford research shows that combined visual + text diagnosis is 23 percentage points more accurate than text-only medical-record analysis. In education, the ability for students to photograph and solve problems has boosted engagement by 40%. In the creative industries, Midjourney, Suno, and Adobe Firefly have, for the first time, given tens of millions of ordinary people professional-grade creative production capabilities.

These are not demo cases, but commercial deployments with a real user base.

From an information-theory standpoint, this trend also has its own internal logic. The volume of video information produced on Earth each day far exceeds that of text. A single frame of 1080P imagery contains information equivalent to thousands of words. An AI that can only process text is exposed to a tiny subset of humanity's information world.

---

## Dimension Two: Is the "Maturity" of LLMs Overstated?

However, the inference that "LLMs are already mature enough, so we should pivot to multimodality" does not hold up under scrutiny.

What is the evidence for "mature enough"? ChatGPT has over 200 million monthly active users, and its enterprise customers cover more than 90% of the Fortune 500. By user scale, this is indeed impressive.

But by reliability of capability, the picture is far more complex. Stanford's 2024 evaluation showed that LLMs still hallucinate at a rate as high as 20-30% in medical-literature summarization tasks. The problems in the legal domain are even more concrete: cases of New York lawyers being sanctioned by courts for submitting fabricated case citations generated by AI keep appearing in the press. Two hundred million users using a tool with such a high error rate every day demonstrates the tool's popularity, not the maturity of its capability.

More importantly, OpenAI's o1/o3 series demonstrated the potential of another path. By abandoning the fast-iteration RLHF route and returning to deep reasoning training, o3's improvement on the ARC-AGI benchmark is several times the sum of all multimodal progress over the past two years. This shows that the reasoning depth of LLMs is far from hitting its ceiling. Pulling resources away from here and redirecting them to multimodality carries a very real cost.

---

## Dimension Three: The Dispute Over Multimodality's Technical Depth

Advocates of multimodality often invoke an exhilarating scenario: GPT-4o looks at a photo and can understand the emotion, analyze the composition, and connect it to context to give a deep response. Isn't that "mature enough"?

Here, there is a key technical detail worth taking seriously.

The architecture of nearly all current multimodal large models is pipeline-style: a vision encoder converts the image into tokens, and the language model processes those tokens. There is no truly bidirectional interaction between visual reasoning and language reasoning—the visual input is ultimately "translated" into linguistic concepts, and the core processing remains language reasoning.

There is an experiment that illustrates this problem: with the same image, if you describe the image's content to GPT-4o in words ("a sad girl, against a backdrop of war ruins"), the quality of its analysis is almost no different from looking at the image directly. The visual input is the "receiving end," and language reasoning is the "processing end"—under the current architecture, the "synergy" of multimodality is largely an illusion. A more accurate description is: the LLM is the core engine, and multimodality is a peripheral input.

A 2024 MIT study also found that most multimodal models exhibit systematic failures in real-world visual reasoning—their understanding of spatial relationships, counting, and 3D structure in particular is markedly weaker than humans'. The physical errors in videos generated by Sora—liquids flowing backward, limbs clipping through objects—make the same point: the model statistically mimics visual patterns, but does not truly understand physical reality.

---

## Dimension Four: The Stratification of Commercial Value

The commercial value of multimodality requires an important distinction.

What are enterprises actually doing with multimodal AI? The core of Salesforce's visual analysis is identifying the product category in an image, HubSpot's image understanding is detecting the frequency with which brand logos appear, and factory safety inspection is identifying whether workers are wearing hard hats. What these tasks truly need is machine vision (computer vision), not multimodal large language models—a ResNet, mature back in 2019, is enough. Most commercial security-AI vendors also use exactly this kind of lightweight, purpose-built vision model, because general-purpose multimodal large models are too costly and too slow at inference.

Andreessen Horowitz's 2024 report corroborates this: most enterprise AI projects scaled back their multimodal features because inference costs exceeded budget. The token cost of multimodal inference is several times higher than that of text inference, which is a real constraint in price-sensitive markets.

There are genuinely high-value scenarios that require the deep capabilities of multimodal large models—complex medical imaging analysis, cross-modal understanding of scientific research data—but these scenarios need domain-specific models even more than general-purpose multimodal large models. In medical-imaging diagnosis, the accuracy of GPT-4V (general-purpose multimodal) is 63%, while a purpose-built medical vision model (the specialized version of Med-Gemini) reaches 84%, and the gap does not narrow significantly as scale increases.

## Dimension Five: The Two-Sided Nature of the Data Bottleneck

Multimodal advocates have an important argument: text data is nearly exhausted. Meta's research team estimates that high-quality text data on the internet will peak in 2026-2028. Image and video data vastly outnumber text and are the way out of the "data wall."

This argument is true, but the chain of logic is incomplete.

Multimodal data is large in volume, but the density of effective training signal is extremely low. Videos on YouTube lack accompanying high-quality semantic annotations, and the alt text for images is extremely crude—multimodal data may be large in volume, but much of it is noise. By contrast, synthetic data is another path for expanding text data—DeepMind's AlphaProof used purely synthetic mathematical-proof data to achieve a historic breakthrough at the International Mathematical Olympiad. In domains with an objective verification mechanism (whether a program runs, whether a proof holds), synthetic data can effectively break through the data wall.

Both paths have their value and their limitations, but directly equating "text data peaking" with "we should prioritize developing multimodality" is a simplification that skips many intermediate steps.

---

## Dimension Six: The Asymmetric Risks of Safety and Ethics

The safety risks of multimodal technology are harder to handle than those of pure text, and are often underestimated.

Deepfakes are the most typical safety hazard of multimodal technology. In 2024, a company in Hong Kong was defrauded of USD 25 million via a multimodally faked video conference. The harmfulness of multimodal content is more covert—context-dependent imagery, cross-lingual visual memes, the spread of prohibited information under the cover of "normal" images—making moderation exponentially harder than for text content.

Content-provenance technologies such as C2PA digital watermarking work in the lab, but fail in real-world distribution environments (transcoding, screenshots, compression). For applications targeting visual emotion recognition, research from the MIT Media Lab further found that the correlation between facial muscle movements and inner emotional states is far lower than commonly assumed, meaning affective-computing applications built on this scientific assumption have inherent accuracy flaws and ethical risks.

This is not a denial of multimodal technology itself, but rather a statement that: at a stage where safety alignment is not yet mature, the rapid expansion of multimodal capabilities calls for more caution, rather than charging ahead on the grounds that "governance will catch up."

---

## Dimension Seven: The Timing Question in Resource Allocation

Taken together, the core of this debate is not "whether multimodality has value," but "at what timing, and in what proportion, to invest in multimodality."

One interesting observation is that there is a clear divergence between what the world's top AI labs focus on in their research publications and what they focus on in their product launches. At the product level, multimodality is the marketing focus and the star of launch events; at the research level, the most profound advances (the reasoning breakthroughs of o1/o3, the systematization of chain-of-thought, self-consistency methods, the verification mechanisms of synthetic data) are mostly in the realm of pure language models.

This divergence reveals something: products move toward multimodality because users can see and touch it and the experience is more intuitive; but the truly hard problems of intelligence are still being dug out by researchers in the soil of language reasoning.

Historically, in the development path of every generation of technology, there has been a stage where "the narrative gets ahead of the technology." Part of the current discussion around multimodal large models falls into this category—its long-term direction may be correct, but its short-term proportion of resource investment may be overestimated by the grand narrative.

---

## Conclusion: Foundation and Roof, Not Either/Or

The most honest answer is neither "multimodality is the future" nor "LLMs are the priority," but rather: both matter, but there is an order.

At the current stage, the reliability, reasoning depth, and safety alignment of language models still deserve the most concentrated resources as the priority direction. These capabilities are the foundation. The perceptual expansion of multimodality is real incremental value, but pouring large amounts of resources into the upper floors while the foundation is unstable carries high structural risk.

This is not technological conservatism, but respect for engineering reality.

The launch of GPT-4o was indeed impressive, and the commercial deployment of multimodality is genuinely advancing. But what truly changes the world is not the feature of "being able to see images," but the as-yet-unrealized whole that emerges when AI can truly integrate perception, reasoning, and reliability. Until that day comes, we need to keep a clear-headed judgment between chasing the narrative and shoring up the foundation.
